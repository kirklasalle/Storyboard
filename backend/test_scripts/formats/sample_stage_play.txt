THE INHERITANCE
A Stage Play in Two Acts

Format: Stage Play
Author: Storyboard AI Demo
Draft: July 3, 2026

================================================================================

CHARACTERS

MARGARET HOLLIS — 72. A woman who built a life by sheer will. She is not
    diminished by age. She is refined by it.

DANIEL HOLLIS — 44. Her son. An architect of other people's futures who
    cannot design his own.

SETTING: The living room of a large, aging house. Once impressive.
    Now carrying the weight of years and unspoken things. A single
    afternoon in autumn.

================================================================================

ACT ONE

SCENE 1

[Lights rise slowly on the living room. Long shadows of late afternoon.
MARGARET sits in an armchair, hands folded, watching the door. She has
been waiting. She is practiced at waiting.

The door opens. DANIEL enters, carrying a briefcase that seems heavier
than its contents. He stops when he sees her. Neither speaks for a moment.]

DANIEL: You said three o'clock.

MARGARET: It is three o'clock. You are late.

DANIEL: The train —

MARGARET: The train has been on time for forty years. Sit down, Daniel.

[He sits. He does not remove his coat. She notices.]

MARGARET: (continuing) You're not staying.

DANIEL: I have a flight at eight.

MARGARET: Of course you do.

[A pause. The clock ticks. Outside, leaves move against the window.]

DANIEL: You said it was important. That there was something to discuss.

MARGARET: There is always something to discuss. We choose not to.

DANIEL: (a short exhale) I drove three hours, Mother.

MARGARET: You flew. From your city, to the nearest airport, then drove
    forty minutes on roads you don't recognize anymore. I know how you
    travel. I know how you live. I know more than you think I know.

DANIEL: Then why am I here?

[She reaches beside her chair and produces a manila envelope. She holds
it out. He doesn't take it immediately.]

MARGARET: Your father left this. He asked me to wait until I was certain
    you were ready.

DANIEL: Ready for what?

MARGARET: For the truth about where the money came from.

[He takes the envelope. His hands are careful, as though it might contain
    something alive.]

SCENE 2

[Later. Daniel has read the documents. They sit between them on the coffee
table. The afternoon light has shifted — longer, colder.]

DANIEL: How long have you known?

MARGARET: Since before you were born.

DANIEL: And you stayed.

MARGARET: (not a question) We all stay.

DANIEL: That's not an answer.

MARGARET: (finally turning to look at him) It is the only answer there is.
    You are an architect. You understand that every structure is built on a
    foundation. You do not have to love the foundation. You do not have to
    admire it. But you must understand it, or the whole thing falls.

[He stands. Walks to the window. The garden outside is going brown.]

DANIEL: The people he hurt—

MARGARET: Cannot be un-hurt. No.

DANIEL: So what do we do?

MARGARET: (a long pause, then quietly) We decide what kind of people we are.
    From here.

[He turns. For the first time, she looks her age. And for the first time,
    he sees it.]

DANIEL: Were you ever happy? With him?

MARGARET: (a complicated, exhausted truth) Yes. That is the hardest part
    of all of it. Yes.

[The afternoon light fades. Neither moves. The clock continues its work.]

BLACKOUT.

END OF ACT ONE

================================================================================

ACT TWO

SCENE 1

[An hour later. The room is the same, but darker. A single lamp on now.
DANIEL has made tea. Two cups. A small act of domestic grace neither of
them acknowledges.]

MARGARET: Your sister called this morning.

DANIEL: I know. I didn't answer.

MARGARET: She'll find out eventually.

DANIEL: About the money?

MARGARET: About everything. She always does.

[A pause.]

DANIEL: I think I should give it back. Whatever can be given back.

MARGARET: (carefully) That would mean lawyers. Attention. Your father's
    name in the papers.

DANIEL: His name is in the papers anyway. In the dedication of the library
    he funded.

[She laughs. Short, unexpected, genuine. The tension shifts.]

MARGARET: You sound like him when you say things like that. He had that
    same impossible accuracy.

DANIEL: Is that a compliment?

MARGARET: It is a fact. You can do with it what you like.

[He sits again. Closer to her this time.]

DANIEL: I don't know what I want.

MARGARET: (quietly, with love) No. You never did. That's why you became
    so good at building what other people want. You have impeccable taste
    for someone else's vision.

DANIEL: (a wound touched) That's a terrible thing to say.

MARGARET: Yes. And true. The two are not mutually exclusive.

[Silence. But a different kind. Something has softened.]

DANIEL: What did you want? When you were young?

MARGARET: (thinking, genuinely) I wanted to be the kind of person who
    could ask that question easily. Without it costing anything.
    I never quite managed it.

[He puts his hand over hers. She doesn't pull away.]

DANIEL: I'll miss the eight o'clock flight.

MARGARET: I know.

DANIEL: Is there anything to eat?

MARGARET: There is always something to eat. I have been waiting for you
    to ask.

[She rises. He watches her go toward the kitchen. The lamp light holds
    the room in amber. Outside, the first stars.]

LIGHTS FADE SLOWLY TO BLACK.

END OF PLAY

================================================================================

PRODUCTION NOTES

This play requires no scenic transitions. The passage of time is conveyed
through lighting shifts only. The director's primary instrument is pace —
the silences are as written as the dialogue and must be honored absolutely.
