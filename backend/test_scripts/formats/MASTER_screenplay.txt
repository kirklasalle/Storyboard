MASTER CLASS: Hollywood Spec Screenplay Format
Every standard element demonstrated through master-level craft.
The story is the teaching tool. Read it as a cinematographer reads —
every action line is a frame. The camera is never mentioned.
The camera is everywhere.

FADE IN:

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: Scene Heading — INT./LOCATION/TIME
================================================================================

INT. AUDIO FORENSICS LAB — NIGHT

The room hums with the specific frequency of machines that do not
sleep. Banks of recording equipment line the walls. Everything is
organized to the edge of compulsion.

DR. NORA VOSS (48) sits at the center console. She is not a
beautiful woman or an ugly one. She is precise. She gave up
softness somewhere around year three of listening to the dead.

A coffee cup. Stone cold. She never noticed it cool.

She hits PLAY.

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: Voice Over — Character (V.O.)
================================================================================

MAN'S VOICE (V.O.)
(from recording, fragmented)
...if you're hearing this, then
I didn't... I couldn't make it
back to —

Static. The waveform on Nora's screen drops to flat.

She rewinds. Plays again. Leans forward until her nose is six
inches from the monitor.

The waveform tells her something the recording does not.

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: Character Introduction — essence over appearance
================================================================================

The door opens. DETECTIVE RAY OKAFOR (50s, the kind of tired that
has learned to look like patience, seventeen years of homicide in
the set of his jaw) enters without knocking. He has earned the
right.

He sets a case file on the desk without disturbing anything else.
He understands Nora's system even if he doesn't share it.

RAY
Preliminary says suicide.

NORA
Preliminary is wrong.

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: Parenthetical — used sparingly, only for
                                 essential performance instruction
================================================================================

RAY
(not dismissing her, considering)
Walk me through it.

Nora points at the waveform on the left monitor. Then the one on
the right.

NORA
This is his voice. His natural
frequency signature — I pulled it
from three years of deposition
recordings. Here.

She points to a section in the right waveform.

NORA (CONT'D)
================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: (CONT'D) — continuation after action break
================================================================================
This is him in the final message.
The fundamental frequency has dropped
eleven hertz. Laryngeal tension.
The kind that happens when a gun is
held against a skull.

A long beat. Ray looks at the waveform. He cannot read waveforms.
He reads the certainty in Nora's body.

RAY
How long have you known?

NORA
Since 3 AM.

RAY
It's 4 AM.

NORA
I know.

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: INSERT — a specific object filling the frame
================================================================================

INSERT — THE PHONE SCREEN

A voicemail timestamp: 11:47 PM. Duration: 0:23. The name above it:
MARCUS BELL. The word beneath: DECEASED.

BACK TO SCENE

Ray picks up the phone with a gloved hand.

RAY
He called someone after this.
At 11:52.

NORA
I know that too.

RAY
Who?

Nora hesitates. This is not her uncertainty about the answer. It
is her reluctance to give it.

NORA
Us. He called the lab.

SUPER: "FIVE HOURS EARLIER"

                                                         FLASHBACK:

INT. AUDIO FORENSICS LAB — EARLIER THAT EVENING

The same room. Less dark. Nora is at her desk, headphones on,
working. The phone on the desk RINGS. She doesn't notice the first
ring. Or the second.

On the third, she pulls off one earphone.

She looks at the caller ID.

She does not recognize the number.

She answers.

NORA
Forensics. Voss.

MARCUS (V.O.)
================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: O.S. vs V.O. — Off-Screen for present action,
                                 Voice Over for narration or recording playback
================================================================================
(hushed, afraid)
Doctor Voss. My name is Marcus Bell.
I've been sending you data. For
three months. Did you — have you
been getting it?

Nora's hand slowly finds a pen.

NORA
Who are you?

MARCUS (V.O.)
I'm the analyst who found what they

I'm the one who should already be dead.

INT. AUDIO FORENSICS LAB — PRESENT — CONTINUOUS

Ray's hand is on his phone. He is already dialing.

INTERCUT WITH:

EXT. MERIDIAN BUILDING — PARKING STRUCTURE — SAME TIME

AGENT KEARN (40s, federal, the specific blankness of a man trained
to have no face) answers on the first ring.

KEARN
You have something.

RAY
Voss has something. I need a warrant
for the Meridian audio archive. All
of it.

KEARN
(a pause that says: no)
That archive is federal evidence.

RAY
Then let's talk about what kind of
evidence it is. And how one of your
confidential informants ended up with
a gun against his head.

The parking structure is so quiet that when Kearn exhales, Ray
hears it clearly.

KEARN
I'll call you back.

He doesn't hang up. He walks to his car. Opens the trunk. Studies
its contents for a moment.

He closes the trunk.

He hangs up.

BACK TO NORA AND RAY:

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: POV — what one character specifically sees
================================================================================

NORA'S POV — THE SECONDARY MONITOR

A spectrogram display. The deleted frequencies in Marcus Bell's
voicemail — the ones that fell below the threshold of standard
playback — reappear as ghost bands in the low register.

They form a pattern. Repeating. Eleven hertz. Thirty-seven
repetitions. A signal buried inside a dying man's last words.

BACK TO SCENE

NORA
He wasn't just telling us what happened.

She starts typing. Her hands are completely steady.

NORA (CONT'D)
He was transmitting.

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: SERIES OF SHOTS — compression of time/action
                                 into visual sequence
================================================================================

SERIES OF SHOTS — THE INVESTIGATION

— Nora's hands moving across three keyboards simultaneously.
— A server address mapping itself across six screens.
— Ray on his phone, pacing, not sitting, never sitting.
— A printer churning out waveform data in long pale sheets.
— Nora's coffee. Still untouched. Now completely forgotten.
— A clock on the wall: 5:14 AM.
— The first grey light against the lab's single high window.
— Nora's face when she finds what she finds.

END OF SERIES

CLOSE ON — Nora's finger poised above the ENTER key.

MATCH CUT TO:

CLOSE ON — Marcus Bell's finger. A photograph. Taken at the scene

BACK TO SCENE

Nora pulls her hand back.

She looks at Ray.

RAY
What?

NORA
He finished what he was sending.
Before they stopped him, he finished
sending it.

RAY
To where?

Nora types a single command. The secondary monitor resolves.

What it shows them fills the room with a silence that has weight
and edges.

The clock on the wall marks the second: 5:21 AM.

Outside, a city begins its indifferent morning.

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: DUAL DIALOGUE — two characters speaking
                                 simultaneously, equal weight
================================================================================

Nora and Ray speak at the same moment. One voice does not dominate.

        NORA                                    RAY
This goes all the way up.          We can't take this to the office.

Neither stops. Neither concedes. The same truth, spoken in two
separate registers, arrives at the same place.

================================================================================
                        ELEMENT: DISSOLVE TO: — soft transition, time passing,
                                 memory, or thematic linkage
================================================================================

DISSOLVE TO:

EXT. MERIDIAN BUILDING — DAWN

The building does not look like a place where terrible things
happen. It is glass and steel and very clean. The morning light
hits it the way light hits things that have no shame.

Nora and Ray stand on the opposite sidewalk. Not yet going in.
Still in the last moment before they are committed to this.

Ray looks at Nora.

NORA
I'm not stopping.

RAY
I know.

A truck passes between them and the building. For three seconds
they cannot see it.

When the truck is gone, the building is still there.

Of course it is.

                        ELEMENT: FADE OUT. — formal close of the scene or
                                 the screenplay
================================================================================

FADE OUT.

                              THE END

SCENE HEADING VARIANTS DEMONSTRATED:
  INT. LOCATION — TIME
  EXT. LOCATION — TIME
  I/E. LOCATION — TIME (interior/exterior simultaneous)
  LOCATION — CONTINUOUS
  LOCATION — LATER
  LOCATION — SAME TIME
  LOCATION — DAWN/DUSK/GOLDEN HOUR/MAGIC HOUR

TRANSITION ELEMENTS:
  FADE IN:                    Opening of screenplay or act
  FADE OUT.                   Close of screenplay or act
  FADE TO BLACK.              Emphatic ending, loss of consciousness
  CUT TO:                     Standard edit (often omitted in modern spec)
  SMASH CUT TO:               Violent or sudden cut — jarring contrast
  DISSOLVE TO:                Soft passage of time or memory
  MATCH CUT TO:               Visual rhyme — same shape, different meaning
  WIPE TO:                    Archaic; period pieces only

SHOT DIRECTIONS:
  CLOSE ON:                   Specific object or feature fills frame
  INSERT                      Discrete close-up; returns to BACK TO SCENE
  ANGLE ON:                   Unnamed specific angle
  POV:                        Subjective — what one character sees
  WIDE ON:                    Establishing or context shot
  INTERCUT WITH:              Two active locations in real-time cross-cut
  BACK TO SCENE               Returning from INSERT or digression
  SERIES OF SHOTS             Montage — compressed time in visual list

CHARACTER CUE VARIANTS:
  CHARACTER NAME              Standard on-screen dialogue
  CHARACTER NAME (V.O.)       Voice-over — narration, thought, recording
  CHARACTER NAME (O.S.)       Off-screen — present in scene, not visible
  CHARACTER NAME (CONT'D)     Dialogue resumes after action interruption
  CHARACTER NAME (PRE-LAP)    Voice heard before the cut completes

SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS:
  SUPER: "TEXT"               Superimposed title on image
  TITLE CARD: "TEXT"          Full title treatment on screen
  FLASHBACK: / END FLASHBACK  Temporal departure and return
  DREAM SEQUENCE /            Subjective, non-reality sequence
  END DREAM SEQUENCE
  TIME CUT                    Unspecified passage of time

ACTION LINE PRINCIPLES (CRAFT):
  — Write what the camera sees, never name the camera
  — One idea per paragraph. White space is pacing.
  — Character introduction states ESSENCE, not appearance
  — Physical behavior over internal state: show the state through the body
  — Sound written into action lines ("The room hums with...")
  — Silence written as physical presence ("A silence that has weight and edges")
  — The world participates: environment reflects or contrasts emotional state
  — Sentences earn their length — short for impact, longer for accumulation

DIALOGUE PRINCIPLES (CRAFT):
  — Parentheticals only for performance direction impossible to read from text
  — Never use parenthetical for emotion: (sadly), (happily) — the line carries it
  — Subtext: characters speak around what they mean
  — Silence inside dialogue: (beat), (long beat), (a moment)
  — Less is more — what is not said is louder
  — Dual dialogue used when two voices must be heard simultaneously, equally
