THE SURVEYOR'S DAUGHTER
A Play in Two Acts

================================================================================
MASTER CLASS: Stage Play Format — Every Element Demonstrated
Format: Full stage play with complete production grammar
Every direction, notation, and structural element is shown in context.
The writing teaches through specificity and restraint.
================================================================================

PLAYWRIGHT'S NOTES

This play requires nothing but two chairs, two actors, and light that
knows how to change. The set, if there is one, should suggest rather
than state. The audience's imagination is the production designer.

The stage directions are written to be read, not just executed. A
director who reads them carefully will find the production inside them.

The play's subject is the inheritance of silence — what parents pass
to children not through words but through the quality of their attention,
their absences, and the specific way they hold things they cannot say.

RUNNING TIME: Approximately 95 minutes with intermission.

================================================================================

CHARACTERS

NORA CLEMENT — 42. An engineer. She builds bridges. The precision required
    for that work lives in every aspect of her person. She does not do
    anything carelessly. This includes loving her father. This includes
    grief, which she is new to and which she is approaching with the same
    rigor she would bring to a structural problem.

LEONARD CLEMENT — 74. Her father. A land surveyor for fifty years. He
    measured the world with instruments and found it, in the end, impossible
    to measure the things that mattered most. He has a diagnosis. He is
    still more present than most people who don't.

    NOTE: Leonard should never be played as a diminished man. He is changed.
    He is still entirely himself in the ways that count.

SETTING

A house that has been lived in for forty years. Not a museum of that
life — a working house, worn in the right places, lit in the specific
amber of late afternoon through old windows.

A single significant prop: a wooden surveying instrument, old, on the
mantelpiece. It has been there for forty years. Nora has always known
what it is. She has never asked where it came from.

================================================================================

ACT ONE

SCENE 1

[Lights rise on the sitting room. Late afternoon. The amber we promised.
LEONARD sits in an armchair, his back to the audience, looking at something
outside the window we cannot see. He has been sitting this way for some time.

NORA enters from upstage right. She carries a bag — work, not luggage.
She is passing through. She had not planned to stop.

She sees her father. She stops.

She sets her bag down — upstage, out of the way. She makes no sound.

She watches him watch the window.

This holds for a full seven seconds. The audience should be made to feel
the weight of this duration. Nora is measuring something.]

NORA: (Quietly, so as not to startle.) Dad.

LEONARD: (He doesn't turn. He knew she was there.) The light does something
    particular today. Have you noticed?

[NORA moves downstage left. Not to him. To a place where she can see
what he sees. She looks at the window.]

NORA: I see it.

LEONARD: It's the angle. This time of year, this time of day. I measured it
    once. Twenty-three degrees off horizontal. It makes everything look
    like it's about to be remembered.

NORA: (A pause. She does not dismiss this.) What were you thinking about?

LEONARD: (Finally turning to look at her.) I was thinking that you came
    because you're worried, and that you're not going to say so, and that
    I'm going to let you not say so, and that we're going to have a perfectly
    good afternoon regardless.

[Nora looks at him.]

NORA: (Simply.) You know me very well.

LEONARD: Forty-two years of study.

================================================================================
        ELEMENT: ASIDE — character addresses audience directly,
                         other characters cannot hear
================================================================================

[Leonard turns back to the window. Nora looks at the audience — not
performing for them, finding them. Speaking from inside the moment,
not outside it.]

NORA: (Aside — quietly, as if thinking aloud.)
    He was diagnosed in February. I flew home in March. And April. This
    is May. I tell myself each visit that I'm going to say what I came to
    say. I have a list. Not a literal list. The kind you carry in the body.

[She turns back. Leonard is still at the window. Nothing has changed
except that we know more now.]

================================================================================
        ELEMENT: Stage Directions — physical geography
                 US = Upstage (away from audience)
                 DS = Downstage (toward audience)
                 SL = Stage Left (actor's left)
                 SR = Stage Right (actor's right)
                 CS = Center Stage
================================================================================

[Nora moves CS. She picks up the surveying instrument from the mantelpiece.
She turns it over in her hands. She has picked this up before — maybe
a hundred times — and always put it back without asking.

Leonard sees her with it in his peripheral vision. He does not turn. He
waits.]

NORA: Where did this come from?

LEONARD: (A long beat before answering.) Your grandfather's house. When
    he died, it was the only thing I took.

NORA: You never told me that.

LEONARD: You never asked.

[A fair point. She holds it.]

NORA: What did he use it for?

LEONARD: He was a surveyor before me. His father before him. It was our
    family's work before it was my work. Three generations of men measuring
    the distance between one place and another.

[He finally turns to look at her. There is something he is moving toward
saying, and he is taking the route he has always taken to difficult
things: the long way around, through the specifics.]

LEONARD: Your grandmother said it was the family curse. That the men in
    our line could measure anything except what was right in front of them.

================================================================================
        ELEMENT: SOLILOQUY — character speaks to themselves, alone onstage
================================================================================

[A knock at the door. Nora goes to answer it. While she is gone, Leonard
is alone. He stands. He moves to CS. He holds the surveying instrument
that she has replaced. He speaks to no one. To the air. To the room.]

LEONARD: (Soliloquy — to himself and to the history of the room.)
    My father never told me he was proud of me. I always thought that meant
    he wasn't. I was fifty-three years old before I understood that it meant
    he didn't know how.

    [He looks at the instrument.]

    I told Nora I was proud of her three times. I remember each one.
    I thought that was enough. I don't think it was enough.

    [He puts the instrument back on the mantelpiece.]

    I have a finite amount of time to make a different accounting.

[NORA returns. Leonard has returned to his chair. She doesn't know what
she missed. The audience does.]

================================================================================
        ELEMENT: Lighting Direction — embedded in stage direction
================================================================================

[The light shifts — the twenty-three degrees Leonard described — and for
a moment the room is all amber and long shadow. The lighting designer
should have been waiting for this moment since the top of the play.
This is their line.]

NORA: (Looking at the light.) Oh. I see what you meant.

LEONARD: There it is.

[The amber holds for ten full seconds.]

NORA: (Very quietly.) I'm worried about you.

LEONARD: (After a moment.) I know.

NORA: I should have said it when I arrived.

LEONARD: You said it when you were ready. That's when things should be said.

INTERMISSION

ACT TWO

SCENE 1

================================================================================
        ELEMENT: Scene setting — time change, physical change
================================================================================

[The same room. LATER. EVENING. The amber has given way to the blue of
dusk and then the darker blue of early night. Two lamps now — the room
is smaller, more intimate. The world outside the window has gone.

Leonard and Nora sit. Between them, on the coffee table, the surveying
instrument. At some point in the interval they have moved it from the
mantelpiece to the table between them. Neither of them may know when.

The conversation has been going on. We arrive in the middle of it,
which is where the real part of any conversation lives.]

LEONARD: The longest survey I ever ran was the eastern boundary of the
    Ardmore land grant. Forty-seven miles. Seventeen days. I was thirty-one.

NORA: I didn't know about that one.

LEONARD: There was a section — day eleven — where I was completely certain
    I'd made an error on day two. Nine days of good work, potentially wrong.

NORA: What did you do?

LEONARD: I went back to day two.

NORA: All the way back.

LEONARD: All the way back.

[A pause.]

NORA: Was there an error?

LEONARD: (A small smile.) No. Day two was right. Everything I'd built on
    it was right. I'd just been standing too close on day eleven to see
    the whole picture.

NORA: (She understands this is not about surveying.) What are you telling me?

LEONARD: (Simply.) That everything you've built on what I gave you —
    you built it right. The foundation was sound. I wasn't always sure,
    watching you. I am sure now.

================================================================================
        ELEMENT: Sound Cue — embedded in stage direction
================================================================================

[Sound: from outside — a low, distant thunder. Not a storm. The suggestion
of one. The sound design should use this sparingly. The thunder should
feel like punctuation, not atmosphere.]

[Nora looks at her father for a long moment.]

NORA: (Her voice doing something unusual — finding the direct route.)
    I need you to tell me things. While you still — while we still have
    time to have these conversations. Things I don't know. Things about
    you, about us. Things you think I know but I don't.

LEONARD: That's a large request.

NORA: I have two days.

[He considers this.]

LEONARD: Then you'd better stay the night.

[This is settled. Not with ceremony. With the simplicity of two people
who have just decided something important and do not need to decorate it.]

NORA: (Standing — moving to SL, to the window.) Tell me about the Ardmore
    survey. From the beginning. Day one.

[Leonard settles into his chair. He finds the posture of a man about to
tell a story he has wanted to tell for a long time.

The lamp light holds them.

Outside, no rain yet. Just the possibility of it.]

LEONARD: Day one. I drove up before dawn so I'd arrive with the light.
    The land was flat in a way that makes you understand why people once
    thought the world was flat — it just goes, in every direction, to the
    horizon, with the specific patience of something that has been here
    much longer than you and will be here much longer still...

================================================================================
        ELEMENT: Sound Cue — scene close
================================================================================

[Sound: The first rain. Not heavy. A beginning. It arrives as Leonard
speaks, as if the sky has been listening and has found, at last, a reason
to respond.

The lights dim slowly. Not to black. To a warm dark. The kind of dark
that contains something continuing.]

BLACKOUT.

END OF PLAY

================================================================================
        ELEMENT: Technical notes — for production
================================================================================

SOUND DESIGN NOTES:
  The thunder in Act Two, Scene 1 should arrive between lines, not under them.
  Rain: real recorded rain, not synthesized. The difference is audible.
  At no point should sound be decorative. Every cue is a word.

LIGHTING NOTES:
  The amber shift in Act One is the most important single lighting moment.
  Everything else in the design serves or echoes it.
  Act Two should feel as though a filter has been removed. The light is cooler,
  truer. The room is the same room seen differently.

STAGING NOTE:
  The surveying instrument must be on the mantelpiece when the audience enters.
  Every audience member who notices it before the play begins is already inside
  the story.

STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS:
  PROLOGUE              Before Act One — sets theme, not story
  ACT ONE / ACT TWO     Primary structural divisions
  SCENE 1 / SCENE 2     Subdivisions within acts
  INTERMISSION          Pause between acts — a structural element, not a break
  EPILOGUE              After final scene — reflection, not resolution
  END OF PLAY           Final notation

CHARACTER DIRECTIONS:
  ASIDE           Character speaks to audience; others cannot hear
  SOLILOQUY       Character alone onstage, speaking to self/air/audience
  PARENTHETICAL   (quietly), (standing), (to himself) — performance note
  Beat / Pause    Duration of silence — beat is shorter, pause is held

  Sound cues — noted within action

PRODUCTION NOTES:
  Playwright's Notes    Opening — intent, approach, performance guidance
  Technical Notes       End of play — sound, lighting, staging specifics

CHARACTER LIST:
  Name — Age. Essence description. Not physical description.
  Physical description is the director's province.
  The playwright provides the interior. The production provides the exterior.

CRAFT PRINCIPLES DEMONSTRATED:
  — Stage directions describe action AND meaning simultaneously
  — Physical geography is emotional geography
  — Asides break the fourth wall surgically, not decoratively
  — Soliloquy requires true solitude — must earn the form
  — Silence is written, not left; a "beat" is a line
  — The set note is the thematic statement
  — What props are onstage before the play begins is dramaturgy
