CHAPTER 1: THE WEIGHT OF STILL IMAGES

The photograph arrived on a Tuesday, which Mira had always considered the
least reliable day of the week. Tuesdays promised nothing, committed to
nothing, and regularly delivered exactly that. The photograph broke the
pattern.

It arrived in an envelope with no return address, the kind of cream
stationery that speaks of old money and older habits. Inside: a single
image, black and white, eight by ten. A woman she had never seen standing
in a garden she recognized immediately.

Her grandmother's garden. Forty years ago. Before it was her grandmother's.

Mira set down her coffee and picked up the photograph with the careful
fingers she reserved for evidence. She had been a documentary filmmaker
for twenty-three years. She knew what a photograph could hide.

The woman in the image was young — perhaps twenty-five — and she was
looking directly at the camera. Not posing. Not performing. Looking at
whoever was behind the lens with the focused, unguarded expression of a
person who has been caught mid-thought and doesn't particularly mind.

There was something in that expression that Mira recognized and could
not name.

She turned the photograph over. On the back, in handwriting she did not
know: For when you're ready to understand what the house was for.

CHAPTER 2: LIGHT ON OLD STONE

The house sat at the end of a lane that the GPS insisted did not exist.
Mira had driven past it three times before she finally pulled over, got
out of the car, and looked properly.

It was smaller than she remembered from childhood visits, the way all
places from childhood are smaller once you bring adult eyes to them.
The garden was overgrown now, the formal paths she remembered barely
visible under years of enthusiastic escape. But the bones of it were
there. The gate. The wall. The peculiar angle of the afternoon light
against the east window that her grandmother had called — without
explanation — "the honest light."

There was a woman sitting on the front step.

Old. Perhaps ninety. Wrapped in a coat that had been expensive once.
She looked at Mira the way people look at something they have been
expecting for a very long time.

"You have the photograph," the woman said. It was not a question.

"You sent it," Mira said. Also not a question.

"Come inside. There are things in this house that have been waiting
to be seen by someone who can see them properly."

Mira looked at the camera bag over her shoulder. Then at the house.
Then at the woman.

"How do you know I can see them properly?"

The woman smiled, and there was the expression from the photograph —
the focused, unguarded, mid-thought gaze.

"Because," said the woman, "you have her eyes."

CHAPTER 3: THE ARCHIVE

Inside, the house smelled of paper and patience. The kind of smell
that only accumulates when someone has been keeping careful account of
things for decades.

The woman — who introduced herself simply as Edna — led Mira to a room
at the back of the house that had clearly been many things before it
became what it was now: an archive. Floor-to-ceiling shelves. Acid-free
boxes labelled with dates and places and names. And at the center,
a table with a projector on it. Old. Reel-to-reel.

"Your grandmother and I made films," Edna said. "Before it was easy.
Before anyone thought women should be doing it. We made fourteen
films together and then we made a choice, and one of us kept filming
and one of us stopped."

"Which one stopped?"

"The one whose name you carry."

Mira sat down heavily. "My grandmother was a filmmaker."

"Your grandmother was a very great filmmaker. And she gave it up for
reasons that were not her own, and she never explained them to anyone,
and she died with that weight, and I think she would want you to
understand why."

Edna threaded the first reel. The projector hummed. On the white wall,
the first image appeared: a woman standing in a garden. Young. Twenty-five.
Looking directly at the camera.

Only now Mira recognized the expression completely.

It was the expression she herself wore when she was filming something
true.

CHAPTER 4: WHAT THE LENS KNEW

They watched for three hours. Fourteen films. Some barely minutes long.
Others approaching half an hour. All of them extraordinary — shot with
the kinetic intelligence of someone who understood not just what to
capture but why, and the devastating precision of someone who had
learned to see the essential moment inside the ordinary one.

Between the fifth and sixth reel, Edna said: "She left everything to me.
The footage. The equipment. The notes. She left strict instructions that
it was not to be seen until someone from the family came who 'understood
what they were looking at.'" She paused. "Four members of your family
have come here before you. None of them were filmmakers."

"What happened?"

"They thought the films were interesting. They did not understand why
the films were necessary."

Mira watched the sixth film begin. Her grandmother — twenty-three, twenty-four,
barely older than a student — handheld camera, running, following a woman
through a marketplace. The woman did not know she was being filmed.
And then she stopped, and turned, and looked straight into the lens with
an expression of absolute fear.

The film cut immediately to black.

"What did she see?" Mira asked.

"That is the question you need to answer," said Edna. "That is what
the house was for."
