THE WITNESS — Stage Play Spectrum
Three levels: Basic → Professional → Oscar / World-Class
Same scene. Same characters. The craft is the difference.

================================================================================
LEVEL 1 — BASIC
================================================================================

A CONVERSATION
A Short Play

CHARACTERS:
  DETECTIVE — A police detective
  WOMAN — A waitress

SETTING: A diner. Night.

================================================================================

[A diner. A DETECTIVE sits at a table. A WOMAN stands near him.]

DETECTIVE: You saw the man?

WOMAN: Yes. He ran out.

DETECTIVE: Did you know him?

WOMAN: No.

DETECTIVE: Are you sure?

WOMAN: Yes. I'm sure.

DETECTIVE: We'll need to talk to you again.

[He leaves. She watches him go.]

END OF LEVEL 1
================================================================================
LEVEL 2 — PROFESSIONAL
================================================================================

THREE A.M.
A Short Play

CHARACTERS:
  DETECTIVE ALAN MARSH — 50s. Has done this a hundred times.
  CAROL — 40s. Has heard a hundred versions of this conversation.

SETTING: A late-night diner. A counter. Two people trying to figure out
how much to say.

================================================================================

[The diner. Fluorescent light. The kind of quiet that happens after midnight.
CAROL wipes down the counter. MARSH sits on a stool, coat on, coffee in front
of him. He has not asked for anything.]

MARSH: He was a regular.

CAROL: [A beat. Decides something.] He came in most nights.

MARSH: Most nights for how long?

CAROL: A year and a half. Give or take.

MARSH: What did you know about him?

CAROL: He liked his coffee black. He read the paper. He didn't talk much
        but when he did he was worth listening to.

MARSH: [Quietly] But you didn't tell Officer Reeves that.

CAROL: Officer Reeves asked if I'd seen anything unusual. I said no.

MARSH: Was that true?

CAROL: [Long pause. She stops wiping the counter.] He ran out. That was unusual.

MARSH: You knew him well enough to notice he was scared.

CAROL: [After a moment] I knew him well enough to know he wasn't a person
        who scared easily.

[Silence. MARSH looks at his coffee.]

MARSH: I'll need you to come in officially. Photos. The whole thing.

CAROL: I know how it works.

MARSH: [Standing] For what it's worth — I don't think you're wrong about him.

CAROL: [Quietly] That's not the same as being right.

[He goes. She looks at the door for a moment. Then goes back to wiping the counter.]

END OF LEVEL 2
================================================================================
LEVEL 3 — OSCAR / WORLD-CLASS
================================================================================

WHAT WE CARRY
A Short Play

CHARACTERS:
  FRANK HARRIS — 54. Homicide detective. Carries the job the way
      people carry an old injury — has learned to move around it
      without calling it what it is.

  MARY CHEN — 48. Has worked the night shift here for eleven years.
      The diner is her country, and she knows its laws.

NOTE TO DIRECTOR: This play has a third character: the empty stool
at the counter's end. It is onstage from the beginning. It should be
lit the way you light something that used to be full.

SETTING: Lena's All-Night. 3:17 A.M. The specific mercy of the late hour.

================================================================================

[The diner. A counter running most of the stage's width. Stools. A jukebox
upstage that everyone knows doesn't work. MARY is behind the counter, polishing
a glass that is already clean. The polishing is not about the glass.

One stool at the end of the counter is empty. The light finds it differently.]

[FRANK HARRIS enters. He moves without hurry. Sits at the counter — not at the
empty stool. Close to it, but not at it. He puts nothing on the counter. Just
his hands.]

HARRIS: You make the pie here?

MARY: My mother's recipe. [A small pause.] She's been gone seven years.

HARRIS: [Taking that in. Not performing sympathy. Actually feeling it.] I'm sorry.

MARY: It was a good seven years of pie since.

[She pours coffee he didn't ask for. He wraps both hands around the cup. The warmth
is real and he lets himself have it. She notices. She does not comment on it.]

HARRIS: You were on when it happened.

MARY: I'm on when everything happens. [She says this without self-pity — as
       simple fact. The night shift is the everything shift.]

HARRIS: The man who left running.

MARY: [The length of a decision, in a half-beat.] He left in a hurry.

HARRIS: He left in a hurry and he didn't pay and you didn't call it in. And when
        the first officer came, you said you'd seen nothing unusual.

[MARY's hands stop. The glass sits on the counter between them. The fluorescents
hum one sustained note.]

HARRIS: [Not accusation. Something adjacent to mercy.] How long?

MARY: [When she speaks, she is testifying to something.] Every Thursday. After ten.
      Four years.

[A breath. She is not looking at the empty stool. She is very specifically not
looking at the empty stool.]

MARY: He ordered medium roast, two sugars. He calculated the tip on his phone —
      always exactly twenty percent. [Beat.] He said thank you like it was a
      complete sentence.

HARRIS: [Quietly] But you knew who he was.

MARY: [The whole scene has been building to this.] I knew who he was in here.

[The distinction between those two sentences — who he was and who he was in here —
lands in the room like something made of glass. Both of them feel it.]

[HARRIS looks at his coffee. Drinks it. Sets it down carefully. The way he sets
it down says: I am not going to make this harder than it already is.]

HARRIS: I'll need a statement. Photographs. We'll make it official.

MARY: I know.

HARRIS: [Standing. And because it's the right thing to ask.] The pie any good?

MARY: [Something that is not quite a smile, not quite forgiveness.] It's the best
      thing I make.

[He leaves money. More than needed. He goes.

MARY stands alone. She picks up the cup HARRIS left. Carries it to the end of the
counter. Sets it down at the empty stool.

She fills it fresh. The steam rises.

The coffee sits at the empty stool, hot and untouched.

The jukebox holds its long silence.

The lights fade — but not all the way. Not on that stool.]

END

================================================================================
STAGE CRAFT NOTES:

Level 1 uses stage directions as traffic instructions.
Level 2 uses them as context and emotional notation.
Level 3 uses them as part of the dramatic text — the direction to the director
about the third character (the empty stool) is as important as any spoken line.

The empty stool is the play's spine. It holds the absent character's moral weight.
Every scene element — where Harris chooses to sit, what Mary doesn't look at,
the coffee placed finally at the empty seat — is in conversation with it.

In great stage writing, the set is a character.
In Level 3, the set is an argument.
