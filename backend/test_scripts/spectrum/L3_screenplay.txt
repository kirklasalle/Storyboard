STORYBOARD AI — QUALITY SPECTRUM: LEVEL 3 (OSCAR / WORLD-CLASS)
Format: Plain Text Screenplay
Story: "The Witness" — A detective questions a diner waitress who saw a murder.

Level 3 is the work where every word earns its place. The scene does not just
tell a story — it is the story, and something more. The reader is changed by
having read it. A great cinematographer reads this and knows every shot before
they've been told a single one.

"Every element is used because the story demands it — not as a syntax exercise."
— ACS4.6

================================================================================

FADE IN:

INT. LENA'S ALL-NIGHT — 3:17 A.M.

The diner exists in the particular mercy of the late hour: fluorescent light that forgives no one, coffee that has been burning since the previous shift, a jukebox unplugged since the nineties that people still put quarters into out of habit.

MARY CHEN (48, third-generation diner, has the kind of face that has heard everything and registered most of it as weather) stands behind the counter polishing a glass that is already clean. The glass is not the point. The motion is.

DETECTIVE FRANK HARRIS (54, the kind of tired that is no longer about sleep) enters without the urgency he feels. Twenty-six years taught him that urgency closes doors. He sits at the counter instead of a booth. Eye level. Not interrogation geometry.

He puts nothing on the counter. No notebook, no badge, no recorder. Just his hands.

HARRIS
(the opener that isn't an opener)
You make the pie here?

MARY
My mother's recipe.

HARRIS
She still baking?

MARY
She's been gone seven years.

Harris nods. Something in him registers this the way it deserves to be registered.

HARRIS
I'm sorry.

MARY
(not dismissing it)
It was a good seven years of pie since.

She pours coffee without asking. He wraps both hands around the mug. Outside, through the window: the yellow tape is still up. They both know it's there. Neither looks at it.

HARRIS
You were on when it happened.

MARY
I'm on when everything happens.
That's what the night shift is.

HARRIS
The man who left running. Black leather jacket.

MARY
(a half-beat — the length of a decision)
He left in a hurry.

HARRIS
He left in a hurry and he didn't pay and
you didn't call it in. And when Officer
Brennan came in, you told him you'd
seen nothing unusual.

Nothing in Mary's face moves. But her hands — the hands still polishing the clean glass — stop.

HARRIS (CONT'D)
(quietly, not accusing — something worse, something like understanding)
How long did he used to come in?

The glass sits still on the counter between them. The fluorescents hum their single note. The jukebox holds its thirty years of silence.

MARY
(the cost of it in every syllable)
His coffee order was medium, two sugars.
He'd been coming in every Thursday
for four years. He always tipped exactly
twenty percent and he always said
thank you like he meant it.

She sets the glass down with a care that is grief by another name.

MARY (CONT'D)
I didn't know what he did
outside of here.

HARRIS
But you knew who he was.

MARY
I knew who he was
in here.

The distinction lands in the room like something breakable.

Harris takes a slow drink of coffee. Sets it down. The way he does it says: I'm not going to make this harder than it already is.

HARRIS
I'll need you to come in. Look at
photographs. Make it official.

MARY
I know.

HARRIS
(standing)
The pie any good?

MARY
(something almost like a smile,
almost like an apology)
It's the best thing I make.

He leaves money on the counter. Enough for three cups. He goes.

Mary stands in the fluorescent mercy of 3:17 in the morning. She picks up the mug Harris was drinking from. Looks at it. Sets it down exactly where his predecessor used to sit.

She fills it with fresh coffee.

It steams on the counter. Waiting for someone who isn't coming back.

FADE TO BLACK.

================================================================================
LEVEL 3 ANALYSIS — The craft at work:

SPECIFICITY:
  "3:17 A.M." — not 3 A.M. The precision signals a real world, not a set.
  "Medium, two sugars. Every Thursday for four years." — four years of Thursdays
  in six words. The grief is proportional and earned.
  "Twenty percent. Always said thank you like he meant it." — character built
  from behavior, not description.

PHYSICALITY AS PSYCHOLOGY:
  Mary's hands polishing a clean glass — displacement activity, controlled anxiety.
  Harris puts nothing on the counter — technique, but also: respect.
  The glass sitting still when she stops — the pause IS the acting note.
  "Sets it down with a care that is grief by another name" — the action line
  does the director's job without stealing the actor's.

DIALOGUE AS ARCHITECTURE:
  "His coffee order was medium, two sugars." — this is not an answer to
  Harris's question. It is more honest than any answer could be.
  "I knew who he was in here." — the distinction between a person's
  public crime and their private grace. The entire moral weight of the
  scene in seven words.
  Harris and Mary never argue, never accuse. The scene's tension is
  the space between what is known and what is said.

IMAGE AS THEME:
  The yellow tape that they both see and neither looks at — complicity
  made visual before a word is spoken.
  The jukebox "unplugged since the nineties that people still put quarters
  into" — the human habit of reaching for comfort that isn't there anymore.
  The final image: a full mug of coffee for someone who will never drink it.
  That is the scene's true last line. The writer knows when words are finished.

THE CINEMATOGRAPHER'S GIFT:
  Every image in Level 3 is a storyboard frame. The DP reads:
  Harris at the counter (eye level, not interrogation angle) —
  the glass stilling on the counter —
  both faces NOT looking at the tape outside the window —
  the final steaming mug at an empty seat.
  Four frames. The whole story.
